About the Dialogue

Background

Migration is a complex and evolving phenomenon shaped by diverse drivers, ranging from economic transitions, demographic pressures, environmental change, insecurity, and global socio-political dynamics. These forces continue to influence internal and international migration patterns creating the need for a responsive and inclusive governance structure.

A 2022 review led by Civil Society Organizations on the 2nd and 3rd levels of the governance structure of the 2015 National Migration Policy further uncovered key weaknesses within these levels of coordination, including inter-agency rivalry, poor information flow, weak feedback mechanisms, and limited capacity for coordination across state and non-state actors. These structural gaps have undermined effective migration management and exposed a significant disconnect between national policy and local realities.

Responding to these dynamics and evolving trends, Nigeria developed the National Migration Policy (NMP) in 2015 as a foundational document to harmonize efforts across sectors and institutions. The policy aimed to address the full spectrum of migration-related issues including labor migration, irregular migration, trafficking in persons, displacement, and diaspora engagement.

The 2025 Revised National Migration Policy

In line with the policy's provision for a five-year review cycle, Nigeria commenced the review process of the 2015 NMP in 2022, incorporating extensive zonal consultations to capture regional peculiarities and trends. A recurring challenge during these engagements was the absence of a state level structure, limiting grassroot participation and creating an urgent need to decentralize migration governance for more context-responsive, inclusive implementation.

The 2024 Civil Society Forum (CSF) on Migration and Development echoed these findings, emphasizing the lack of structured coordination at the state level and identifying local funding gaps as a persistent barrier to localized migration responses. These insights shaped a critical outcome of the revised policy.

"The Revised National Migration Policy, 2025 addresses these identified challenges and gaps by introducing a fourth level of governance, state-level coordination structure, providing a framework for formally integrating state and local actors into the national migration governance."

This inclusion demonstrates Nigeria's commitment to the whole-of-government and whole-of-society approach while emphasizing decentralization, bottom-up participation, ensuring that migration governance is closer to the grassroot.

South East Relevance

The South East region of Nigeria is a primary axis of mobility in West Africa. Known for the industriousness of its people, experiencing high rates of emigration, both internal (to Lagos, Abuja, Kano) and international majorly Europe, North America, and Asia. While this mobility drives significant remittance inflows vital to the region's economy, it creates critical governance challenges.

High Migration Propensity

Nigeria remains the top recipient of remittances in Sub-Saharan Africa, with a significant portion flowing to the Southeast. Anambra, Imo, and Abia states are among the top origins for international passport applications.

Irregular Migration Risks

South Eastern states are consistent 'sending areas' for both regular and irregular migrants, with a 'youth bulge' where over 60% of the population is under 30, facing high unemployment.

Brain Drain vs. Brain Gain

The region continues to lose critical mass in the health and education sectors. There is currently no unified sub-national policy to engage the high-skilled diaspora.

Subnational Governance Gap

State and local governments lack the legal mandate, institutional capacity, data systems, and dedicated resources to effectively manage migration's local drivers and impacts.

Objectives of the Zonal Dialogue

The South East Zonal Migration Dialogue aims to:

  • 1

    Familiarize migration actors with the validated 2025 National Migration Policy (NMP), ensuring broad understanding and grassroot engagement

  • 2

    Translate national and international migration policy frameworks into regionally relevant strategies that respond to specific drivers and patterns in the South East

  • 3

    Engage in strategic advocacy with relevant government bodies contributing to the formal adoption of the validated policy

  • 4

    Improve understanding and usage of migration data at sub-national levels to better plan for mobility trends, reintegration needs, labor market impacts, and diaspora engagement

  • 5

    Develop CSO-led roadmaps aimed at initiating and institutionalizing decentralized migration governance across south east states, tailored to local migration dynamics and capacities

  • 6

    Strengthen collaboration and coordination among state-level government actors, civil society organizations, private sector stakeholders and diaspora networks

  • 7

    Promote sustainable local funding mechanisms by actively engaging the private sector, particularly at the grassroots level
